Optics: Waves & Rays

Light bends, scatters, and interferes. We can describe it as waves reflecting off barriers or as rays taking the path of absolute least time across refractive boundaries.

1  Fermat's Principle of Least Time

How does light know to bend at a boundary? Pierre de Fermat solved this in 1662: light takes the path that takes the least time. Since light travels faster in air (n₁ = 1) than in water (n₂ = 1.5), it spends more distance in the fast medium and less in the slow one. The path that minimizes total time is bent, and the math yields Snell's Law (n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂) exactly.

2  Young's Double-Slit Wave Interference

When light waves pass through two slits, they spread out and overlap. Where two wave crests meet, they reinforce (constructive interference, bright fringe). Where a crest meets a trough, they cancel (destructive interference, dark fringe). This interference creates a series of bright and dark fringes on a screen.
