Chapter 4

Newton's Three Laws

What does it take to keep something moving? Aristotle said a continuous push, and every rolling ball seems to agree. It took Galileo's imagination to see past friction — and Newton to build the whole of mechanics on what he saw.

1  Galileo's ramps

Release a ball on one incline and let it roll down and up a facing incline: it climbs back to (very nearly) its starting height. Galileo reasoned it would reach exactly that height if friction could be removed. Now make the second incline shallower — the ball must roll farther to regain its height. And if the second incline is perfectly flat? The ball still "tries" to regain its height, but never can. It must roll forever.

Flatten the second ramp
The dashed line marks the starting height. Try 20°, then 8°, then 0°.

Nothing pushes the ball on the flat track — and nothing stops it. That is Newton's first law: force is not what maintains motion but what changes it. A body at constant velocity is no more mysterious than a body at rest.

2  Equal force, unequal effect

If force changes motion, how much change does a given force buy? Newton's second law answers: the acceleration equals the net force divided by the mass, F = ma. Mass is the resistance to being accelerated — inertia, measured in kilograms.

His third law adds a twist that fools almost everyone: when two bodies interact, the forces on each are always equal and opposite — a speeding truck and the mosquito it hits exert exactly equal forces on each other. What differs is what those equal forces do.

Same force, different mass
both bodies feel the same 100 N force

Equal forces, unequal consequences: the same 100 N gives the light body a thousand times the acceleration when it has a thousandth of the mass. Do not confuse the force with what the force does.

3  The Atwood machine

In 1784 George Atwood faced a problem: free fall is too fast to measure with an eighteenth-century clock. His fix was sly — hang two nearly equal masses over a pulley. Only the difference of the weights drives the motion, but the whole combined mass must be accelerated, so the system falls in slow motion: a = (m₁ − m₂)g / (m₁ + m₂).

With 6 kg and 4 kg the acceleration is exactly g/5 — free fall slowed five-fold, gentle enough for a pendulum clock. Atwood measured g this way to about one percent. The full analysis — free-body diagrams, and why the tension always sits between the two weights — is in the textbook chapter.
