Chapter 1

Vectors

Some quantities are single numbers. Others carry a direction with them — and adding those requires new rules, in which 4 + 3 can come out anywhere from 1 to 7. This chapter builds the single most important mathematical tool of the course.

1  Broken arithmetic

Walk 4 m, stop, then walk 3 m more. How far are you from where you started? You cannot answer — and that is the point. It depends on which direction the second walk took. The total distance you covered is always 7 m; your displacement — the straight-line shortcut from start to finish — can be anything from 1 m to 7 m.

Drag the slider. At what angle is the displacement exactly 5 m? 1 m? 7 m?

Quantities that obey ordinary arithmetic — distance, mass, time, temperature — are called scalars. Quantities that carry a direction — displacement, velocity, force — are vectors, drawn as arrows: length for magnitude, arrowhead for direction.

2  Adding arrows

That forces and velocities combine like walks — direction and all — is a discovery about nature, first worked out by the Flemish engineer Simon Stevin in 1586. The rule: place the tail of the second arrow at the head of the first; the sum runs from the start of the first to the tip of the second. From the geometry, two arrows of lengths a and b at angle θ add up to a resultant of magnitude R = √(a² + 2ab cos θ + b²).

Rotate the locked arrows and watch the resultant: parallel gives 7, opposite gives 1, and exactly 5 demands a right angle — because cos θ must vanish. The broken arithmetic of the first section is fixed: the angle decides.

3  Components

Working with arrows geometrically gets clumsy fast. The professional trick is to split every vector into perpendicular components — its shadows on the coordinate axes. A vector of magnitude A at angle θ above the x-axis has components Ax = A cos θ and Ay = A sin θ; and Pythagoras returns the magnitude: A = √(Ax² + Ay²).

Once vectors become pairs (or triples) of numbers, every operation turns into ordinary arithmetic on like components: addition adds them, and the dot and cross products get one-line formulas.
