Chapter 3

Motion

Zeno argued an arrow frozen at one instant is indistinguishable from an arrow at rest — so what does "90 km/h at this moment" even mean? This chapter builds the missing idea: the derivative, invented for exactly this problem.

1  Speed at an instant

Average velocity is honest but blunt: displacement divided by elapsed time. To get the speed right now, we shrink the stopwatch window. Take a falling ball, s = 5t², and ask how fast it moves at t = 2 s. Measure over a window Δt after that moment, and you get an average; make Δt smaller and the average settles toward one definite number.

Shrink the window: secant becomes tangent
average velocity over the window:
The line through the two measurement points is a secant. As Δt → 0 the two points merge and the secant becomes the tangent — its slope, 20 m/s, is the instantaneous velocity. That limit is the derivative: v = ds/dt.

This is the answer to Zeno: motion at an instant is not a property of one frozen frame, but of how neighbouring instants compare — taken to the limit. For s = 5t² the limit works out to v = 10t, and at t = 2 s that is exactly 20 m/s.

2  The three graphs

Position, velocity, acceleration: each graph is the slope of the one above it. Reading this triple fluently is the core skill of kinematics — and it is trained by predicting. Pick a motion, sketch the three graphs on paper, then reveal.

Predict, then reveal

Watch for the classic trap in "ball thrown straight up": at the top the velocity passes through zero, but its slope — the acceleration — stays constant at −10 m/s². If it were zero there, the ball would hang in the air forever.

3  Two motions at once

Put two coins at a table's edge. Drop one; at the same instant, flick the other horizontally. Which lands first? Almost everyone predicts the dropped one — commit to your own answer before pressing release.

Drop one, flick the other

They land together — always, at any flick speed. Gravity does not care about horizontal motion: the vertical fall proceeds identically whether the coin moves sideways or not. Projectile motion is just two independent motions happening at once — constant velocity horizontally, free fall vertically — which is why the one-dimensional tools above solve it completely. The full treatment (range, maximum height, the parabola) is in the textbook chapter.
