1  Friction: stick and slip

Push a heavy box gently and it does not move — friction pushes back with exactly the force you apply. Push harder: it matches you again. Static friction is not a fixed number but an adjustable constraint, up to a maximum μs𝒩. Beyond that threshold the box slips, and the friction drops to a fixed kinetic value μk𝒩 — slightly less than the static maximum, which is why boxes lurch when they finally let go.

Push the box (m = 10 kg, μs = 0.5, μk = 0.4)
Increase the push slowly and watch the friction bar track yours exactly — until 50 N. Leonardo da Vinci measured these laws five hundred years ago; his surprise: the contact area does not matter.

The two facts worth keeping: friction is proportional to how hard the surfaces press together (the normal force 𝒩), and independent of contact area — a brick dragged flat or on edge needs the same pull.

2  The elevator

A bathroom scale does not measure gravity — it measures the normal force 𝒩 pressing on it, and displays 𝒩/g. Standing still, 𝒩 = mg and the two agree, so the distinction hides. An elevator exposes it: Newton's second law gives 𝒩 = m(g + a), so the reading rises when the elevator accelerates upward, falls when it accelerates downward — and drops to zero if the cable snaps, even though gravity never switched off. Weightlessness is the absence of the normal force, not of gravity.

Note the case people argue about longest: cruising upward at steady speed reads exactly 60.0 — the scale responds to acceleration, never to velocity. And astronauts float for the snapped-cable reason: the station and everyone in it are permanently falling, together.

3  Newton's cannonball

Newton's own thought experiment, from the Principia: fire a cannonball horizontally from a high mountain. Too slow, and it falls to the ground. Faster, and it falls farther before landing. Fast enough — about 7.9 km/s — and the ground curves away exactly as fast as the ball falls: it misses the Earth forever. That is what an orbit is. The Moon is doing nothing else.

Fire the cannonball
Try 5, then 7.9, then 11.5. Somewhere near 11.2 km/s the ball stops coming back at all — escape velocity.

The same inverse-square law does all of it: the apple's fall, the cannonball's arc, the Moon's orbit, the tides. Newton checked it with a number — the Moon "falls" with exactly 1/3600 of the apple's acceleration, at 60 times the distance — and you can redo his arithmetic in the textbook chapter, along with Kepler's laws, Cavendish's weighing of the Earth, and why the Bay of Fundy breathes sixteen metres twice a day.
