Chapter 2

Dimensional Analysis

Every physical quantity is built from a few base ingredients — mass [M], length [L], time [T]. Any honest equation must balance these on both sides. That single rule catches algebra mistakes, and sometimes predicts a formula before any physics is done.

1  Guess the formula

Here is a challenge that sounds impossible: predict how a pendulum's period depends on its length l, its bob's mass m, and gravity g — without using any law of motion. Assume the answer is a product, t = k la mb gc, and demand only that the dimensions work out to a time.

Balance the dimensions: make a time
t  =  k · l? · m? · g?
dimensions:
Reminders: [l] = L, [m] = M, [g] = L T−2. You need the total to come out as plain T.

There is exactly one way to win — and notice what the winning setting says: the mass exponent must be zero. A heavy stone and a light stone on equal strings must swing in step, not because anyone measured it, but because no other answer can balance. Two stones and a shoelace will confirm it. (What the method cannot give you is the dimensionless constant k; the full theory says k = 2π.)

2  The bomb in the photograph

In 1947 the US government declassified photographs of the first atomic fireball, each frame stamped with a time and a distance scale. The yield was still top secret. The British physicist G. I. Taylor reasoned that the fireball radius R could depend only on the energy E, the air density ρ, and the time t — and there is exactly one way to build a length from those: R = (E t²/ρ)1/5.

Fit the energy to the photograph
The dot is the declassified data: R ≈ 140 m at t = 25 ms. Slide E until the curve passes through it — you are re-deriving a military secret with bookkeeping.

Taylor published his estimate in 1950: about twenty kilotons, embarrassingly close to the classified value. No espionage — just the same exponent-balancing you did in the previous section. The full story, and the method's limits, are in the textbook chapter.
